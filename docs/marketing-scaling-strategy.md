### 1. THE DREAM BUYER PERSONA

**Profile: Ramzi, 42, Beirut**

Owner of a 3-7 agent real estate agency. Has been in the business for 8-15 years. Started as an agent himself, built the agency through relationships and hustle. His listings are on Property Finder and Bayut. His WhatsApp never stops buzzing.

---

**CORE FEARS**

- A faster competitor is responding to the same lead right now, while his agent is asleep or busy
- He is paying 5 agents and still missing deals he cannot account for
- Property Finder leads cost him $300-600/month and he has no idea what ROI he is actually getting
- A newer, tech-forward competitor will eventually eat his lunch
- His best agent might quit tomorrow, and half the client relationships leave with them
- He is getting older and busier, but he cannot step back because the business falls apart when he does

**CORE FRUSTRATIONS**

- Agents respond to WhatsApp leads whenever they feel like it, not within the 5-minute window that actually closes deals
- 30-40% of inbound leads come in after 9pm or before 9am, and nobody is responding
- Every morning he wakes up to 3-5 leads from the night before that are now cold
- He spends half his day following up on leads that his agents forgot to follow up on
- His agents spend the majority of their time on unqualified tire-kickers who will never buy
- He has no visibility into what his agents are actually saying to leads on WhatsApp
- He has tried hiring a receptionist to manage leads and it costs more than it produces
- WhatsApp Business is basically just his regular WhatsApp with a catalog feature he never uses

**SECRET DESIRES**

- To be known as the most professional, most responsive agency in his area
- To sleep at night knowing leads are being handled without him
- To scale his agency from 5 to 20 agents without the chaos multiplying linearly
- To look more sophisticated and tech-forward than competitors without hiring a developer
- To stop babysitting agents and focus on high-value deals
- To have data and proof that his marketing spend is actually working
- To have his agency run itself well enough that he can take a real vacation

**DAILY REALITY**

7:30am: Opens WhatsApp. Three cold leads from last night that nobody handled.
9:00am: Calls the agent who missed them. Agent says "I saw them late, I'll follow up now." Too late.
11:00am: Attends a property viewing. Phone buzzing with two new leads nobody is handling.
2:00pm: Reviews the CRM (if he even has one). Half the fields are empty.
5:00pm: One of his agents closes a deal. Today was good.
8:00pm: New leads coming in. No agents working. He personally responds to two of them.
11:00pm: Sees three more leads. Too tired to respond. Sets a reminder to follow up tomorrow morning.

**THE NUMBER THAT HITS HIM HARDEST**

If his agency gets 60 leads per month, misses 25% due to slow or no response, and closes 10% of qualified leads at an average commission of $3,000: he is losing $4,500 per month in commissions. Every month. That is $54,000 per year vaporizing because of a response-time problem.

**THE OBJECTIONS HE WILL RAISE**

1. "My agents respond fast enough." (They do not, and he knows it)
2. "Lebanese clients do not trust bots." (They are not talking to a bot, they are texting on WhatsApp)
3. "This is too technical for us." (It is WhatsApp, they already use it all day)
4. "We are not ready to implement this right now." (Translation: convince me this is worth 30 minutes)
5. "What if it says the wrong thing?" (He has full control, all responses are reviewed and approved)
6. "$600/month is expensive." (One commission covers 6-12 months of subscription)

### 1. SETUP FEE + MONTHLY VS PURE SAAS

**Ottley's AAA model context:** Phase 1 AI agency clients pay $3K-$10K upfront implementation fee plus $1K-$3K monthly retainer. The upfront fee funds the build, the retainer funds the maintenance and results delivery.

**The case for a hybrid model for Wakeeli:**

Pure SaaS (no setup fee) works when:
- The product self-onboards in under 30 minutes
- No custom configuration is needed
- The market is digital-native and comfortable with credit card subscriptions

Lebanese real estate agencies in 2026 are not that market. They need:
- WhatsApp Business API account setup (requires Meta Business verification)
- AI trained on their specific listings, neighborhoods, and agency tone
- Integration testing with their existing workflow
- Agent training on the dashboard

This is real work that takes 3-5 hours per client. A setup fee covers that work AND signals seriousness. Agencies that pay a setup fee churn at dramatically lower rates because they have already committed.

**Recommended pricing structure:**

Setup fee: $1,500 (one-time, covers WhatsApp API setup, AI training on their listings, 2 onboarding calls)
Monthly: Standard tiers ($149 / $599 / $1,200)

**The positioning script:** "There is a one-time setup cost of $1,500 that covers everything we need to configure specifically for your agency. After that, the monthly subscription runs itself. Most agencies recover the setup cost in the first deal Wakeeli books for them."

**Revenue impact on path to $30K MRR:**

If Fox signs 5 agencies per month at Professional tier:
- Month 1: 5 agencies x $1,500 setup + $599/mo = $7,500 + $2,995 MRR
- Month 2: 5 more = additional $7,500 + $5,990 MRR total
- Month 3: 5 more = additional $7,500 + $8,985 MRR total
- Month 4: 5 more = additional $7,500 + $11,980 MRR total

The setup fees essentially fund the operation while MRR compounds. This is cleaner than burn-and-scale.

**Important caveat:** The setup fee should eventually be waived or reduced as the product becomes more self-serve. In year 2, if onboarding is under 2 hours, drop the fee and compete on pure SaaS. For now, the setup fee is correct.

### 2. OTTLEY'S CLIENT ACQUISITION APPLIED TO LEBANESE BUSINESSES

Ottley's core acquisition playbook: outreach + demo + case study loop.

**Social proof is everything:** In Lebanon, who you know matters as much as what you do. One respected agency owner vouching for Wakeeli to his peers is worth more than $5,000 in Facebook ads. Build a referral culture from day one.

**What requires adaptation for Lebanon:**

- Cold email is largely useless. WhatsApp outreach is primary.

1. WhatsApp outreach to Dream 100 agencies (using HVCO as the opener)

3. Demo closes to setup fee + first month

### 3. NICHE SELECTION: IS LEBANESE REAL ESTATE THE RIGHT SCOPE?

**Three options evaluated:**

**Option A: Lebanese real estate only (current focus)**

Pros:
- Fox has studied actual transcripts from 7 real agencies
- Deep product-market fit with a specific, validated pain
- The WhatsApp lead flow in Lebanese real estate is well-understood
- Easier to build social proof: "We work with Lebanese real estate agencies specifically"
- High commission business means $599/mo is easy to justify
- First client references will be highly relevant to the next prospect

Cons:
- Total addressable market is capped (Lebanon has roughly 300-500 credible real estate agencies)
- Economic fragility: Lebanese market is still recovering post-2019 crisis

**Option B: MENA real estate**

Pros:
- Massive market expansion (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Jordan all have large real estate sectors)
- Higher price tolerance especially in Gulf markets
- UAE specifically is a logical next step after Lebanon: same WhatsApp culture, higher commissions, more sophisticated agencies

Cons:
- Fox does not have the same insider knowledge of UAE or Saudi real estate workflows
- Competitive landscape in UAE is more developed
- Regulatory and operational complexity of setting up in multiple jurisdictions

**Option C: All Lebanese businesses with WhatsApp lead flow**

Pros:
- The problem is not unique to real estate. Car dealers, medical clinics, travel agencies, interior designers, all have the same WhatsApp lead problem.
- Larger TAM within Lebanon

Cons:
- Different lead qualification logic for each vertical
- No specialization story to tell prospects
- Harder to build a compelling case study ecosystem

**Recommendation: Lebanese real estate through month 6. MENA real estate starting month 7.**

The reason is simple: depth beats breadth in the early stages. Specialization creates a story. "The AI conversion system built specifically for Lebanese real estate agencies" is a more powerful proposition than "AI for businesses that use WhatsApp." Stay in the niche, own it completely, then expand.

The MENA expansion path is clear: use the Lebanese case studies to penetrate the UAE market. UAE real estate agencies are larger, charge higher commissions, and will pay Enterprise pricing without hesitation if the ROI case is proven.

Martell's 95-5 Rule: 5% of your activities drive 95% of your results. Everything else is noise.

**Fox's 5% for Wakeeli in months 1-4:**

**Getting the first 10 agencies to visible, measurable success.**

- Digital agencies see real results and want to recommend Wakeeli to their clients (Stack 5)

The tactical implication: in months 1 and 2, Fox should be obsessively focused on two things only. Getting agencies signed up, and making sure those agencies see results fast enough that they become evangelists.

**Everything that does not serve those two goals is the 95%: ignore, automate, or delegate it.**

### 4. CHURN PREVENTION: WHAT KEEPS AGENCIES SUBSCRIBED

Churn is the killer of SaaS math. At $30K MRR, 10% monthly churn means spending the entire next month replacing what was lost, not growing.

**The three types of churn and how to prevent each:**

**Type 1: Value Churn (They cancel because they did not see results)**

Prevention: The onboarding experience must produce a visible result within 14 days. Not a vague "getting set up" update. A concrete result: "In your first 14 days, Wakeeli handled 23 conversations and booked 4 tours. Here is the breakdown."

Build the 14-day success moment into the product. If an agency has not booked 2+ tours in 14 days, Fox gets an automatic alert and calls them personally.

**Type 2: Attention Churn (They forget they have it, then cancel when they see the charge)**

Prevention: Monthly performance reports sent automatically, showing cumulative value delivered. The report headline should always be a number: "Wakeeli saved your agency 47 hours of agent time this month."

The report creates a monthly moment where the client actively appreciates the value they are getting. Without it, subscriptions become invisible and vulnerable.

**Type 3: Competitive Churn (A competitor offers a lower price)**

Prevention: Switching costs and data lock-in. Once an agency's lead history, conversation data, and client profiles are in Wakeeli, leaving means losing that history. Build for this intentionally. The dashboard should make the data feel owned and valuable, not just operational.

Additionally, the WhatsApp Business number association creates practical lock-in. If their business WhatsApp runs through Wakeeli's platform, switching providers requires re-verifying their number through Meta, which is a multi-week process they will not want to go through.

**Retention Benchmarks to Target:**

- Month 1-3: Focus on zero churn from the founding cohort. These clients must succeed.
- Month 4 onwards: Target 95%+ monthly retention (5% monthly churn or lower)
- Year 1 goal: 90%+ annual retention

**The Retention Conversation:**

At the 60-day mark, Fox should have a personal call with every client. Not a support call. A growth conversation: "Where are you now vs where you were 60 days ago? What would make Wakeeli even more valuable to you over the next 6 months?"

This call does three things: prevents quiet churn, surfaces upgrade opportunities, and generates authentic testimonial material.

### 5. CAPITAL DECISION: BOOTSTRAP VS RAISE

**The Martell framework:** Raise money to pour fuel on a proven fire, not to build the fire itself. Raising before product-market fit is confirmed just means expensive experimentation with other people's money.

**Current situation analysis:**

- Product exists and is technically functional
- 0 paying clients today
- No validated churn rate
- No proven CAC (customer acquisition cost)
- No replicable sales process yet

**Verdict: Bootstrap hard through month 4 ($30K MRR target). Do not raise before this.**

Here is the math on why bootstrap works here:

Path to $30K MRR via setup fees + subscriptions (as modeled above):
- 5 new agencies/month at Professional tier
- Each month generates $1,500 x 5 = $7,500 in setup fees
- By month 4, setup fees alone have funded $30,000 in operations
- MRR compounds independently of this

The setup fee model makes Wakeeli self-funding. Fox does not need outside capital to reach $30K MRR. He needs sales execution.

**When to consider raising:**

If Fox hits $30K MRR in 4 months and wants to expand into UAE or Saudi Arabia within 6 months, a $300K-500K pre-seed round makes sense. The pitch becomes easy: proven model in Lebanon, established case studies, known CAC, clear expansion playbook for MENA.

At that point, raise to pour fuel on the fire. Not before.

**What bootstrap requires:**

- Fox's personal time as the primary input in months 1-2
- Setup fees fund the part-time technical contractor
- Keep monthly operational expenses under $2,000 (server, ads, tools) until MRR validates higher spend
- No office, no unnecessary hires, no overhead that does not directly drive revenue
